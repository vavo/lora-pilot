# Data Rights and Consent

_Last updated: 2026-07-06_

Training data has a boring question attached to it: are you allowed to use these images this way? Boring questions become expensive when ignored.

This page is practical documentation, not legal advice. It gives you the checks a beginner should run before turning a folder of images into a dataset.

## The Short Version

Use images you own, images you created, commissioned work with training rights, public domain material, or images with licenses that allow your use. Keep notes beside the dataset: source URL, creator, license, download date, and any restrictions.

Do not treat "found on Google" as permission. Google Images finds files; it does not grant rights. The same goes for screenshots, social posts, portfolio images, marketplace previews, brand photos, and random Pinterest archaeology. The internet is not a consent form.

## People, Products, and Styles

Images of real people need extra care. You may need consent, model releases, or internal approval, especially for commercial use, employee photos, client work, medical contexts, children, private events, or anything that could imply endorsement.

Brands and products can bring trademark, confidentiality, and contract issues. A product LoRA trained on your own catalog is different from a product LoRA trained from scraped marketplace images. Keep that distinction visible in the dataset notes.

Living artists' styles deserve caution. Even when a use is technically allowed in your jurisdiction, training a style LoRA on a living artist's work without permission can create reputational and platform risk. Use licensed, commissioned, public domain, or self-created style examples when you can.

## Safer Dataset Sources

- Your own photos or renders.
- Commissioned work where the contract allows AI training.
- Images generated by tools whose terms allow reuse for training.
- Public domain material.
- Creative Commons material that allows your planned use.
- Stock or licensed libraries with terms that permit model training.

[Creative Commons Search](https://search.creativecommons.org/) and [Openverse](https://openverse.org/) can help find CC-licensed and public-domain media. [Unsplash](https://unsplash.com/license) allows broad free use under its own license, but you still need to watch for recognizable people, brands, trademarks, and the site's restrictions.

## TagPilot Habit

Create a small `SOURCES.md` or `sources.csv` file before you start captioning in TagPilot. Add one row per image or image batch. That habit feels tedious for about five minutes. It becomes useful the first time you need to remove a source, explain a dataset to a client, or reuse the LoRA six months later without guessing where the training data came from.
